Treatment-course assumption

The base case counts one Ixo-vec treatment course per modeled treated eye, with any future repeat-course layer dependent on whether clinical follow-up establishes a routine retreatment interval. The current clinical anchor is reduced supplemental anti-VEGF injection burden after Ixo-vec.

Clinical anchor: Adverum LUNA/OPTIC 52-week and 4-year update reported an 88% reduction in annualized injections and 54% injection-free status for LUNA 6E10 through 52 weeks, plus 86% annualized anti-VEGF reduction in OPTIC through year 4.

Calculation variable key

Subscript labels such as I1 identify intermediate values used in the worked 2036 examples.

IAvailable source eyes

Annual high-burden branded anti-VEGF source eyes before eligibility, access, adoption, and Ixo-vec allocation are applied.

I12036 beginning untreated clinical pool = 87,405 eyes. This is the start-of-2036 balance, not the original model-start pool. The 2025 pool begins at 106,950 eyes, calculated as A 1,150,000 active treated eyes x B 20% high-burden share x 62% branded-source share x 75% clinical-eligibility filter. Each year then subtracts treated eyes and 6% attrition while adding new inflow and fellow-eye conversions; the 2035 ending balance becomes the 2036 beginning pool.
I2New clinical inflow during 2036 = 8,342 eyes. This equals 200,000 incident wet AMD patients x 78% treatment initiation x 1.15 eyes per treated patient x 10% high-burden incident share x 62% branded-source share x 75% clinical-eligibility filter.
I3Fellow-eye conversions during 2036 = 2,226 eyes. This equals (19,717 opening at-risk fellow-eye stock + 2,539 new at-risk fellow eyes from I2 / 1.15 eyes per patient x 35% at-risk share) x 10% annual fellow-eye conversion rate.

2036 example: I available source eyes = I1 87,405 + I2 8,342 + I3 2,226 = 97,972 available source eyes.

JAccess multiplier

Access is modeled in two pieces: a peak access ceiling and a year-specific ramp toward that ceiling.

J1Peak access assumption = 85%. This is the mature share of considered eyes expected to be reachable after coverage, site readiness, and patient access constraints.
J22036 access ramp = 100%. This means the model has reached the peak access ceiling by 2036; it is a timing multiplier, not a higher access ceiling after 2036.

2036 example: J access multiplier = J1 85% peak access x J2 100% access ramp = 85%.

KEligible and accessible eyes

Available source eyes after the treatment-considered pool factor and access are applied.

2036 example: I 97,972 available source eyes x D 65% pool considered x J 85% access multiplier = 54,130 eligible and accessible eyes.

LAnnual adoption ramp

Year-specific physician adoption and patient-selection ramp for the durable-treatment option.

L12036 base-case adoption schedule value = 100%

2036 example: L annual adoption ramp = L1 100%.

MDurable-option use

Eligible and accessible eyes expected to receive a durable-treatment option before assigning share to Ixo-vec.

2036 example: K 54,130 eligible and accessible eyes x F 70% durable-treatment adoption x L 100% adoption ramp = 37,891 durable-option use.

NIxo-vec treated eyes

Durable-option use allocated to Ixo-vec after within-class competition is applied.

2036 example: M 37,891 durable-option use x E 35% Ixo-vec allocation = 13,262 Ixo-vec treated eyes.

OAnnual price index

Year-specific US net-price multiplier used to model price erosion after peak years.

O12036 US net-price index = 1.00x

2036 example: O annual price index = O1 1.00x, meaning no modeled US net-price erosion is applied in 2036.

US revenue in millions of dollars after treated eyes, net revenue per treated eye, and annual price index are applied.

P1Core segment US sales = 13,262 treated eyes x $90,000 x 1.00x / 1,000,000 = $1,194M
P2Other modeled US source segments = $15M

2036 example: P1 $1,194M core segment + P2 $15M other US source segments = P $1,209M total US sales.

Modeled ex-US net-price multiplier versus US net revenue per treated eye.

Q12036 ex-US price index = 0.50x

Eligibility factor

Share of the high-burden patient/eye pool considered for possible durable-treatment use before access, adoption, and competition are applied.

Durable-treatment class

Wet AMD options chosen primarily for longer treatment durability, including gene therapies such as Ixo-vec, higher-dose anti-VEGF products such as Eylea HD, longer-interval biologics such as Vabysmo, and implant or sustained-delivery approaches. Ixo-vec receives a modeled allocation within this class.

Branded anti-VEGF source

Eyes currently treated with branded anti-VEGF agents such as Eylea, Vabysmo, Lucentis, and similar products. Net revenue per Ixo-vec-treated eye is modeled separately.

2036 patient funnel

This example uses the base-case high-burden branded anti-VEGF source segment. It shows how the model narrows from clinically available eyes to accessible eligible eyes, durable-option use, and finally Ixo-vec-treated eyes.

Source eyes

High-burden eyes currently sourced from branded anti-VEGF treatment, such as Eylea, Vabysmo, Lucentis, or similar branded products, before the model applies durable-option consideration, access, adoption, and competition.

Considered, eligible, and accessible

Considered eyes are judged plausible for longer-duration treatment. Eligible and accessible eyes are the considered eyes that remain reachable after clinical suitability, payer/site access, and that year's access ramp are applied.

Durable option

A longer-duration wet AMD treatment choice. The class is broader than gene therapy: it can include Ixo-vec or other gene therapies, longer-interval biologics such as Vabysmo, higher-dose anti-VEGF such as Eylea HD, and sustained-delivery or implant approaches.

Ixo-vec and other branches

Ixo-vec treated eyes are the modeled share of durable-option use assigned to Ixo-vec after durable competitors. Other durable options are durable treatments not assigned to Ixo-vec; other management means accessible eyes that do not move to a durable option in this step.

High-burden pool considered

Share of high-burden branded anti-VEGF source eyes judged plausible for durable-option use in that year before access and adoption are applied.

Access ramp

Year-specific share of peak access reached in that year. It phases in coverage, site readiness, and practical patient access; 1.00 means peak access has been reached.

Adoption ramp

Year-specific physician and patient uptake of durable options after access. It reflects clinical comfort, patient selection, and launch learning over time.

Price index

Multiplier on modeled US net revenue per treated eye. 1.00 means no modeled net-price erosion; values below 1.00 reduce revenue per treated eye.

Ex-US uptake

Year-specific launch and uptake multiplier for explicit non-US sales after the modeled ex-US delay.

How the math works

Each year repeats the same calculation logic. The model starts with a clinical opportunity pool, adjusts it for that year's eligibility and access, applies capture and competitive share, then converts treated eyes into US and worldwide sales.

01
Start with available eyes

A Active treated eyes and B high-burden share anchor I available source eyes before annual inflow and fellow-eye conversions.

02
Apply eligibility and access

C initial or D mature pool considered x J access multiplier produces K eligible and accessible eyes.

03
Apply durable adoption

K eligible and accessible eyes x F durable adoption x L annual adoption ramp produces M durable-option use.

04
Adjust for durable competitors

M durable-option use x E Ixo-vec allocation produces N Ixo-vec treated eyes.

05
Convert eyes to US sales

N treated eyes x G net revenue per treated eye x O annual price index / 1,000,000 produces P US sales in millions.

06
Add explicit ex-US sales

P US sales x H ex-US sales factor x Q ex-US price index x R annual ex-US uptake contributes to S worldwide sales.

Plain language explanation

The model asks: How many wet AMD eyes could realistically become eligible for a durable option in a given year, what share physicians would actually treat, what portion of durable-treatment use goes to Ixo-vec, what Ixo-vec earns per treated eye, and how much non-US sales should be added after launch delay. The answer changes every year because eligibility, adoption, access, price, competition, and ex-US availability change over time.

The base case is driven almost entirely by high-burden eyes sourced from current branded anti-VEGF use. Low-cost and lower-burden source segments are included, but they remain small in this version of the model.

Why this matters

If a reviewer disagrees with the forecast, the fastest way to pressure-test the model is to challenge the high-burden branded anti-VEGF source assumptions first: eligible-pool size, durable-class adoption, Ixo-vec allocation within the durable-treatment class, and net revenue per treated eye. Those variables explain most of the base-case revenue.

Model assumptions

The model separates sourced evidence, triangulated estimates, and explicit assumptions because many commercial forecasting variables are unavailable in model-ready public datasets.

Directly anchored evidence

Adverum management's Schedule 14D-9 projections, the CVR $1B annual worldwide net-sales hurdle, and market context inputs are sourced. These are reader-verifiable anchors; source eyes, eligible/access, durable-option use, Ixo-vec treated eyes, and sales are still calculated by the model logic.

Triangulated estimates

Active treated eyes, branded/low-cost source shares, high-burden share, and lower-burden expansion use public context plus model judgment to form source-eye pools before eligible/access and durable-option filters.

Adjustable model inputs

Net revenue per treated eye, ex-US uptake, competitor allocation, eligible-pool expansion, and adoption ramps control how considered eyes become eligible/access, move to durable-option use, and convert to US/ex-US sales.
